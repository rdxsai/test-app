## 1. objective_text

Explain the structure of WCAG 2.2, including the POUR principles, guidelines, success criteria, and conformance levels A, AA, and AAA.

## 2. plain_language_goal

Help the learner understand how WCAG 2.2 is organized: what the four high-level principles are, how guidelines fit under them, what success criteria are, and how the A/AA/AAA conformance levels work together.

## 3. mastery_definition

A learner has mastered this objective when they can:
- Correctly place a given WCAG item (e.g., "1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum)") into the hierarchy: principle -> guideline -> success criterion.
- Explain in their own words the role of principles vs guidelines vs success criteria (i.e., intent vs non-testable guidance vs testable requirements).
- Identify the conformance level of a success criterion (A, AA, or AAA) and state what meeting "AA" entails regarding "A" and "AAA."
- Describe, without error, the relationship between conformance levels and success criteria (e.g., to conform at Level AA, all Level A and AA success criteria must be met for the scope).
- Interpret a WCAG 2.2 success criterion identifier (e.g., 2.5.8) and map it to its principle and guideline.

## 4. objective_type

- hierarchy/structure
- conceptual understanding
- classification
- comparison
- decision-making (limited, about conformance level implications)
- debugging/evaluation (limited, about spotting misclassification of elements)

## 5. prerequisite_knowledge

**Essential prerequisites:**
- Basic idea of web accessibility and why it matters (users with disabilities, barriers, inclusive design).
- What "standard/guideline" documents are and why organizations follow them.
- Very basic web context: what "web content" means and that a "page/view" can be evaluated.

**Helpful but nonessential prerequisites:**
- Familiarity with an example WCAG 2.x success criterion.
- Awareness that WCAG has versions (2.0, 2.1, 2.2) and is technology-agnostic.
- Basic idea of testable vs non-testable statements.

## 6. prerequisite_gap_policy

If the learner lacks essential prerequisites:
- Pause and run a brief micro-lesson: define web accessibility in a sentence, define what a standard is, and give a concrete example of "web content."
- Use one quick diagnostic example (e.g., "Text must have sufficient contrast" as a testable requirement) to establish testable vs non-testable.
- Keep it under 3 minutes, then return to the main objective.

## 7. concept_decomposition

1. Purpose of WCAG as a standard for web content accessibility.
2. High-level organization: principles -> guidelines -> success criteria.
3. POUR principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust (as categories, not detailed content).
4. Guidelines: descriptive, non-testable guidance under each principle.
5. Success criteria (SC): testable statements under guidelines; each has an identifier, title, and level.
6. Identifier structure: principle.guideline.SC numbering (e.g., 1.4.3).
7. Conformance levels: A, AA, AAA definitions at a high level.
8. Roll-up rule: Level AA conformance requires meeting all A and AA SC; AAA is additional.
9. Scope of conformance: conformance applies to defined web pages/content (not partial elements in isolation).
10. Distinction: techniques/advisory notes vs required success criteria (techniques are not required for conformance).
11. Version context: "WCAG 2.2" label and that its structure mirrors 2.x family.

## 8. dependency_order

1. **Purpose of WCAG as a standard** -> establishes why structure matters.
2. **High-level organization** (principles -> guidelines -> success criteria) -> gives the overall map before details.
3. **POUR principles as categories** -> anchors the top layer to four memorable buckets.
4. **Guidelines as non-testable statements** -> clarifies the middle layer's role.
5. **Success criteria as testable requirements** -> clarifies the bottom layer's role; introduce identifier format (e.g., 1.4.3).
6. **Conformance levels A/AA/AAA and roll-up rule** -> defines how requirements translate to claims.
7. **Scope of conformance** (applies to a defined set of pages/content) -> prevents misapplication; clarifies what "conform" refers to.
8. **Distinguish success criteria from techniques/advisories** -> avoids common confusion about what is required.
9. **Version context for 2.2 within 2.x** -> situates learning without deep-diving into version diffs.

This sequence moves from purpose to map to layers, then to how conformance is determined and claimed, minimizing cognitive load by establishing categories before details and separating structure from implementation specifics.

## 9. likely_misconceptions

- Believing guidelines are testable or have levels; confusing guidelines with success criteria.
- Thinking principles have levels (A/AA/AAA) or are requirements.
- Assuming meeting a Level AA SC alone means AA conformance without meeting all Level A SC.
- Believing techniques are mandatory for conformance (confusing "sufficient techniques" with requirements).
- Assuming WCAG conformance applies automatically to any part of a site rather than to a defined scope.
- Confusing WCAG 2.2 with earlier versions or WCAG 3; assuming the structure changed fundamentally.
- Misreading the SC numbering as priority or difficulty rather than placement.
- Thinking AAA criteria are required for typical conformance claims.

## 11. socratic_question_goals

- **Purpose of WCAG**: diagnose prior knowledge; connect motivation to structure.
- **High-level organization**: force comparison of categories vs requirements; check hierarchical reasoning.
- **POUR principles**: check classification understanding; reveal misconception that principles are rules.
- **Guidelines**: reveal misconception about testability; justify why guidelines exist if not testable.
- **Success criteria**: check causal understanding of how requirements are verified; interpret identifier components.
- **Identifier structure**: test parsing and mapping to hierarchy.
- **Conformance levels**: justify choice (what must be met for a given level); check roll-up understanding.
- **Scope of conformance**: diagnose understanding of what a conformance claim covers.
- **SC vs techniques**: reveal misconception that techniques are mandatory; check reasoning about alternatives.
- **Version context**: confirm they can anchor to "2.2" and not mix with "3.0."

## 12. example_requirements

**Simplest introductory example:**
- A single success criterion such as "1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) — Level AA" with its placement under Principle 1 (Perceivable) -> Guideline 1.4 Distinguishable.

**Contrastive examples:**
- A principle statement (e.g., "Perceivable") vs a guideline (e.g., "1.4 Distinguishable") vs a SC (e.g., "1.4.6 Contrast (Enhanced) — AAA") presented side-by-side to classify.
- A success criterion vs a technique (e.g., "Use relative units for text" as a technique) to distinguish required vs optional methods.

**Borderline/tricky cases:**
- A Level AAA success criterion (e.g., "2.4.9 Link Purpose (Link Only) — AAA") to discuss why AAA is not required for AA conformance.
- An SC newly added in 2.2 (e.g., "2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) — AA") to show version context without deep-diving into content.
- A scenario where a site meets most but not all A SC: can it claim AA? (Answer should expose the roll-up rule.)

**Transfer scenarios:**
- Given an unfamiliar SC identifier (e.g., "3.2.6 Consistent Help — A"), have the learner place it under the right principle and infer implications for conformance at different levels.
- Given an organizational goal "We aim for Level AA," ask which SC levels they need to meet and how to scope a claim.

## 13. retrieval_requirements

**Must-retrieve:**
- Official WCAG 2.2 outline: list of the four principles and their names.
- A minimal structure example of one principle -> one guideline -> one SC, including the SC's identifier, title, and level.
- Definitions/roles of "principle," "guideline," "success criterion," "conformance level" in WCAG 2.2 context.
- Official description of conformance levels A/AA/AAA and the rule that AA requires all A and AA SC (and that AAA is optional).
- Explanation that techniques/advisories are non-normative and not required for conformance.
- A clear description of conformance scope (e.g., applies to web pages/sets of pages/content).
- At least one SC newly introduced in 2.2 with its identifier and level for version context (no deep content needed).

**Optional-supporting:**
- A simple visual hierarchy diagram (textual outline acceptable) mapping principle -> guidelines -> SC.
- Examples of common organizational conformance targets (e.g., "AA") to contextualize.
- Short note that numbering (e.g., 1.4.3) indicates placement, not priority.
- Links to W3C Understanding/Quick Reference pages for deeper exploration (for advanced learners).

## 14. assessment_evidence

**Quick understanding checks:**
- Multiple-choice: classify "1.4 Distinguishable" as principle/guideline/success criterion.
- Short answer: What does "AA" mean in "1.4.3 — AA"?
- Parse "2.1.1 Keyboard" into principle number and level.

**Application task:**
- Given "2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) — AA," place it under the correct principle and guideline; state what conformance levels must include it.
- Provide two items (e.g., a principle and an SC) and ask the learner to order them by hierarchy and explain roles.

**Transfer task:**
- Organization aims for Level AA conformance for a product section. Ask the learner to describe which SC levels must be met and what the defined scope should look like.
- Given an unfamiliar SC identifier, have the learner determine its principle and conformance implications.

**Misconception check:**
- True/False: "Guidelines have conformance levels." (False)
- True/False: "You can claim Level AA conformance if you meet all AA SC but miss one A SC." (False)
- Short answer: Are techniques required to conform? (No; explain why briefly.)

## 15. adaptation_notes

**Beginner:**
- Spend more time on the purpose and the three-layer map before conformance details.
- Use a single running example across all layers to reduce split attention.
- Avoid multiple SC examples at first; add only one AAA contrast later.
- Provide more scaffolded classification questions.

**Intermediate learner:**
- Introduce two or three SC across different principles to practice mapping and level identification.
- Add scope-of-conformance scenarios early.
- Briefly highlight one 2.2 addition to reinforce version context.

**Advanced learner:**
- Start with a diagnostic: give a mixed list of principles, guidelines, SC, and techniques; ask them to sort and justify quickly.
- Introduce edge cases in conformance scope (e.g., subsets of a site, components reused across pages) without deep legal/technical detail.
- Compare 2.1 vs 2.2 structurally (no deep dive into changes) to ensure they can situate 2.2.

## 16. boundaries_and_non_goals

- Do not teach the detailed content of specific success criteria (definitions, measurements, test procedures).
- Do not cover techniques in depth or how to implement them in code.
- Do not address legal compliance or policy mandates in specific jurisdictions.
- Do not teach WCAG 3 or Silver project details beyond noting different versioning.
- Do not cover non-web documents or native apps accessibility standards beyond acknowledging WCAG's focus on web content.
- Do not cover evaluation methodologies (e.g., sampling, testing protocols) or authoring tools/user agents specifics.

## 17. concise_plan_summary

- Start by motivating the purpose of WCAG and present the three-layer hierarchy framed by the four POUR principles.
- Clarify roles: principles (categories), guidelines (non-testable guidance), success criteria (testable requirements).
- Teach how to read SC identifiers and map them back to principle/guideline.
- Introduce conformance levels A/AA/AAA and the roll-up rule; explain scope of conformance claims.
- Use contrastive examples to separate principles/guidelines/SC and SC vs techniques.
- Include one simple SC example and one AAA contrast; optionally include a 2.2-specific SC for version context.
- Check understanding with classification, mapping, and conformance implication questions; include a misconception check.
- Adapt depth and number of examples to learner level while keeping the core structure focus unchanged.
